  Discussion:
  With higher variance the two Gaussian distributions overlap more.
  Points near the decision boundary (roughly the line x+y = 10)
  can legitimately belong to either distribution.  K-Means draws
  a linear Voronoi boundary between centroids; samples on the
  'wrong' side are labelled as misclassified, but they are not
  truly wrong — they simply lie in the overlap region where
  both clusters have non-negligible probability.

  [plot] Saved: outputs/digit_centroids_kmeans.png

  Note on centroid visualisation:
  Each centroid pixel value is the mean of the binary feature
  across all samples assigned to that cluster.  This equals the
  probability that the pixel is ON for a random pattern in the
  cluster.  Because digits within a cluster share a common shape,
  the centroid looks like a blurred (probabilistic) version of
  the digit — bright where the stroke usually appears, dark
  where the background is.

  Discussion:
  Best test accuracy: 96.47% at k=200

  As k increases, training accuracy improves because more
  clusters can capture finer group structure.  However, test
  accuracy eventually plateaus or drops — classic overfitting.
  Over-specialised clusters fit noise in the training set and
  fail to generalise.  X-Means mitigates this via BIC-based
  stopping, automatically choosing a moderate k.
